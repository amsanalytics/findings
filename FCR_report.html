The Floor Coverage Ratio

A peer-intelligence metric for performing arts centers · AMS Analytics · Q2 2026 · internal

This note introduces a metric we built and validated from the cohort filings — the Floor Coverage Ratio — and shows what it can tell a center's leadership. It's written to be read; the full methodology and evidence live in a companion technical reference.

What it measures

Every center carries a fixed cost just to keep its building open — occupancy plus administration, what the platform calls its operating cost. The Floor Coverage Ratio asks one question: after a center's Broadway series pays for itself (its artists, production, and marketing), how much of that fixed building cost does what's left over cover?

1.0 — Broadway covers the building outright
0.5 — it covers half
2.0 — it covers the building twice over

Because the number is already net of what Broadway costs to run, it represents real, discretionary money — not gross ticket sales. Across the clean cohort, Broadway expense eats 60–95¢ of every Broadway dollar; the ratio is built on what's left.

The cohort at a glance

Two things stand out, and together they're why the metric is worth trusting:

  • It separates centers cleanly. They fan out across five natural bands — from centers whose Broadway carries the building one-and-a-half times over, down to those where it covers essentially none of it. That's structure, not noise.
  • Each center has a stable "normal." Most boxes are narrow — a center sits where it sits, year after year. The occasional long whisker to the right (Seattle, Bushnell, Tennessee) is a single blockbuster season that snaps back, not a new altitude. That persistence is what lets a center treat its median as a real baseline.

And the dashed 1.0 line tells its own story: only about a quarter of centers cover their building outright on Broadway. For most, the typical season covers about half.

What it's for — a lens, not a scorecard

A higher ratio isn't a better grade; it's a different position. The number measures how self-sufficient a center's building is on its commercial programming — and the room above that coverage is what's available to spend on mission programming that was never meant to pay for itself. Two centers at opposite ends of the chart aren't winner and loser; they're different institutions making different bets. The value to a leader is twofold: knowing where they actually stand relative to peers, and holding a single, legible number that explains to a board why the commercial slate is what underwrites everything else.

Reading your own number over time

The cohort view shows the metric works; the real use is watching one center across years. Two examples.

How to read a move

  • A one-year jump out of a center's normal range is almost always a single blockbuster title, and it reverts. Don't bank it as the new baseline.
  • A sustained move to a new level is structural — usually the building cost grew faster than the programming could follow. That's the number to act on.

And because the metric is sticky year to year, next season's honest expectation is a center's recent level, not its best season — which is why the projection on each chart sits deliberately below the center's lifetime peak.

What it doesn't tell you

It's a structural screen, not a verdict. It doesn't speak to within-season timing, it doesn't assign which program "owes" the shared building cost, and it reads differently for a center that owns its hall than one that rents. Read it as a position over time, not a report card.
